Forests

Forests clean the air, regulate the climate, and are home to thousands of species. They help retain moisture and protect soils.

Interesting fact

Features

Most of us only see trees in the forest, but underground there is a real ‚Äúnatural internet.‚Äù Through a vast network of fungal mycelium (mycorrhiza), trees communicate, exchange sugars, and warn each other of danger. When one tree is attacked by insects, it releases chemical signals, and the entire forest around it begins to produce protective toxins.

Steppes

Steppes have fertile soils and play an important role in food production.

Interesting fact

Features

The steppe is not a wasteland, but a real ‚Äúupside-down forest.‚Äù While trees reach for the sky, steppe grasses hide 90% of their biomass underground. Their root system is so dense and deep that it can hold more carbon in the soil than tropical jungles, turning the steppe into the most powerful natural shield against global warming.

Mountains

Mountain regions provide fresh water and are home to rare species. They are highly sensitive to climate change.

Interesting fact

Features

Mountains are not just rocks, but real ‚Äúwater towers‚Äù and climate regulators of the planet. They occupy only a quarter of the land, but provide fresh water to more than half of humanity, storing it in glaciers and high-altitude lakes. Due to the sharp change in altitude, mountains contain several natural zones at once: climbing to the top, you can travel from dense forests to the Arctic tundra in a single day.

Deserts

Despite the harsh conditions, deserts are home to unique plants and animals that have adapted to the heat and lack of water.

Interesting fact

Features

The desert is not dead sand, but a space of extreme natural intelligence. While we see endless dunes, beneath them there is a bustling life that has learned to cheat death: plant seeds here can wait decades for rain to bloom overnight. At the same time, they are the main suppliers of minerals for the whole world: for example, dust from the Sahara flies across the ocean and becomes fertilizer for the Amazon forests, proving that even the most arid places are critically important links in the global ecosystem.

Tundra

The tundra is a cold ecosystem with unique natural features. It reacts quickly to global warming, which threatens its inhabitants.

Interesting fact

Features

The tundra is the Earth's ‚Äúrefrigerator,‚Äù where life exists in maximum energy-saving mode. Due to the permafrost, trees here do not grow upwards, but spread along the ground. This is one of the most important ecosystems for the climate: the frozen soil of the tundra has been storing huge reserves of carbon for millennia, preventing the planet from overheating. Despite the severe cold, in summer the tundra turns into a giant oasis of thousands of lakes, where millions of birds from all over the world flock to raise their young in safety and peace.
